Reading philosophical literature for advanced students

Thu, Jul 20, 2017

What is philosophy?

Philosophy is the study of problems that are fundamental and highly general. Wilfred Sellars wrote in 1960:

The aim of philosophy, abstractly formulated, is to understand how things in the broadest possible sense of the term hang together in the broadest possible sense of the term.

There’s something to Sellars’ definition of philosophy—though it is also a funny thing to say, precisely because it says so little!

Philosophers study problems that concern, for example, what reality is, how our beliefs are justified, how mind and body can interact, what the necessary conditions for a just society are, and what value is.

Philosophy in Cambridge

The main style of philosophy candidates will encounter at Cambridge is known as ‘analytic’. Analytic philosophy currently is the dominant philosophical tradition in the English-speaking world (it is typically contrasted with ‘continental’ philosophy, but the distinction is neither clear nor very helpful).

Analytic philosophy is marked by a method that focuses on clarity and prefers simplicity over all-encompassing philosophical systems. Moreover, a confidence in logical reasoning and the natural sciences is typical for the analytic tradition. You will find an emphasis on both these aspects in the Cambridge Philosophy Tripos, though there certainly is room for reading works in other traditions.

To read

The following list is a selection of works that reflects the topics candidates will cover during their studies for the Philosophy Tripos at Cambridge. There is no need to read all of this. The aim of this list is to give you a sense of the variety of topics a student at Cambridge will come across.

Metaphysics and Mind

  • Thomas Nagel ‘What is it like to be a bat?’ The Philosophical Review (1974)
  • Jennifer Hornsby, ‘Agency and actions’ Royal Institute of Philosophy Supplement (2004).

Ethics and Political Philosophy

  • Judith Thomson ‘The Trolley Problem’ The Yale Law Journal (1985).
  • Susan Wolf, ‘Moral Saints’, Journal of Philosophy (1982).

Logic

  • Dorothy Edginton, ‘The philosophical problem of vagueness’ (2001).
  • Bertrand Russell, ‘On Denoting’ (1905).

Historical Texts

  • Plato, Apology (Available online)
  • George Berkeley, Three Dialogues between Hylas and Philonous (1713) (Available online)
  • David Hume, ‘Of the Standard of Taste’ (1757)

To listen

Perhaps you are curious to find out about current philosophical debates. I can recommend listening to some of the bite-sized interviews with contemporary philosophers made available on Philosophy Bites: philosophybites.com

For a historical perspective on philosophy, you can listen to the History of Philosophy (without any gaps!) podcast produced by Professor Peter Adamson (LMU, Munich): historyofphilosophy.net

To watch

The Royal Institute of Philosophy has a good archive of lectures delivered to their society. You can find them on the Royal Institute of Philosophy YouTube Channel.
