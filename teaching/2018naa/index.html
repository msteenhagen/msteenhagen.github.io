Necessity, Analyticity, A Priority

Sun, Oct 7, 2018 Part 1a, Meaning, Michaelmas 2018

Course description

This course will introduce several key distinctions: analytic / synthetic; a priori / a posteriori; and necessary / contingent. It will cover the syllabus materials on Analyticity, A Priority, and Necessity (the first section of Paper 3, Meaning). Feel free to contact me at ms2416@cam.ac.uk if you have any suggestions, questions, or comments about this course.

General reading

An interesting and readable introductory textbook is: Sybil Wolfram (1989), Philosophical Logic: An Introduction, Oxford: Routledge.

Online resources

Outlines and handouts will be made available on: http://msteenhagen.github.io/teaching/2017plo/

Where and when

Monday 3-4pm. Lecture Block Room 1

Lecture 1: Necessity

What is necessity? Can we distinguish different forms of necessity? This lecture discusses the notion of a necessary truth, and presents a model for such truths using possible worlds. (slides)

Suggested Reading
  • W.V.O. Quine (1956), ‘Quantifiers and propositional attitudes’ The Journal of Philosophy 53, 177-187.
  • W.V.O. Quine (1943), ‘Notes on existence and necessity’ The Journal of Philosophy 40, 113-127

Lecture 2: Analyticity and A priority

What explains the necessity of truths? This lecture continues the discussion of necessary truth, and considers whether a statement’s analyticity is an adequate explanation of necessity. This leads to a classic empiricist view that necessity, analyticity and a priority stand or fall together.

Suggested Reading
  • A. J. Ayer (1946), Language, Truth and Logic (2nd ed.), London: Gollancz, ch. 4.

Lecture 3: Necessary but synthetic truths

Can empirical truths be necessary? This lecture introduces Kripke’s notion of a ‘rigid designator’, and considers the possibility of necessary but a posteriori truths.

For the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, Joseph LaPorte wrote a good and detailed discussion of rigid designators. As is usual with the SEP, the entry contains clear introductions of the main ideas, followed by increasingly advanced material. I don’t expect you to be able to follow all of it (yet), but I can recommend reading at least the introduction (§1) and section (§3): https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/rigid-designators/

Suggested Reading
  • Saul A. Kripke (1971), ‘Identity and Necessity,’ in M.K. Munitz (ed.), Identity and Individuation. New York: New York University Press. 135-164. PDF

Lecture 4: Reference and description

This lecture continues our discussion of rigid designators, and considers the possibility of a priori truths that are contingent.

Suggested Reading
  • Saul A. Kripke (1971), ‘A Priori Knowledge, Necessity, and Contingency’, in P.K. Moser, ed., A Priori Knowledge (Oxford: Oxford University Press, 1987), ch. 7. (Excerpt of his Naming and Necessity)
