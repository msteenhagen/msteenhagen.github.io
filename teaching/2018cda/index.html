Conditionals

Mon, Nov 5, 2018 Part Ia, Meaning, Michaelmas 2018

Course description

These lectures will introduce the philosophical discussion about conditionals. For this, we need to ask what the relation is between the meaning of formal languages and the meaning of natural languages.

Online resources

Outlines and handouts will be made available on:

http://msteenhagen.github.io/teaching/2018cda/

Where and when

Monday 3-4pm. Lecture Block Room 1.

Lecture 1: Meaning and natural language

This lecture will discuss Grice’s theory of sentence meaning and communicative intentions.

(Download handout here)

Suggested reading

H.P. Grice (1957), ‘Meaning’, The Philosophical Review, Vol. 66, No. 3. (Jul., 1957), pp. 377-388.

Lecture 2: Logic and conversation

This lecture will expand on Grice’s theory of sentence meaning, by considering the role and behaviour of conventional and conversational implicature.

(Download handout here)

Suggested reading

H. P. Grice (1957), ‘Logic and Conversation’ in Peter Cole and Jerry L. Morgan (eds.) Syntax and Semantics, Vol. 3, Speech Acts, New York: Academic Press 1975 (Also in Grice’s Studies in the Ways of Words)

Lecture 3: Paradoxes of natural language

This lecture will introduce the paradoxes of conditionals, which occur when we try to transition between natural and formal languages. We will try to resolve these paradoxes by using Grice’s theory of implicature.

(Download handout here)

Suggested reading

Mark Sainsbury (2001), ‘The Project of Formalization,’ (ch. 6) in Logical Forms: An Introduction to Philosophical Logic, Oxford: Blackwell, 339-391.

Lecture 4: Conditionals: two approaches approaches

In this lecture we will look at problems with the Gricean solution to the paradoxes of conditionals, and consider an alternative approach

(Download handout here)

Suggested reading

Dorothy Edgington (2008), ‘Conditionals’, in E.N. Zalta, ed., Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (Winter Edition) [Online].
