Consciousness

Sun, Sep 30, 2018 Part II, Philosophy of Mind, Michaelmas 2018

Course description

In this series of advanced seminars we will discuss six key contributions to the philosophical debate about the nature of consciousness. The reading list covers the Part II syllabus materials on Consciousness for the Tripos paper in Philosophy of Mind. Besides Part II students, students on the Philosophy MPhil programme are welcome to attend.

Feel free to contact me at ms2416@cam.ac.uk if you have any suggestions, questions, or comments about this course.

Reading requirement

The first reading listed for each week is required, any further readings are optional.

Online resources

Information about this course will be made available on: http://msteenhagen.github.io/teaching/2018con/

Where and when

Thursday 11:00-12:00. Lecture Block Room 10

Session 1: Phenomenal Consciousness and Access Consciousness

Required Reading

Ned Block (1995), ‘On a Confusion About a Function of Consciousness’, Behavioral and Brain Sciences, Vol. 18, No. 2, pp. 227-47.

Suggested Reading

Daniel Dennett (1981), ‘Towards a Cognitive Theory of Consciousness’, in Brainstorms. Brighton: Harvester, pp. 149-73.

David Rosenthal (1986), ‘Two Concepts of Consciousness’, Philosophical Studies, Vol. 49, pp. 329-59.

Session 2: Phenomenal Consciousness and Iconic Memory

Required Reading

Ian Phillips (2011), ‘Perception and Iconic Memory: What Sperling Doesn’t Show’, Mind & Language, Vol. 26, pp. 381-411.

Suggested Reading

G. Sperling (1960), ‘The information available in brief visual presentations’, Psychological Monographs, Vol. 74, No. 11.

Barry Dainton, ‘Temporal consciousness.’ The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy. Fall 2010 ed. Ed. Edward N. Zalta. https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/consciousness-temporal/

Session 3: The Hard Problem of Consciousness

Required Reading

David Chalmers (1996), The Conscious Mind. Oxford: Oxford University Press. Selection tba.

Suggested Reading

Thomas Nagel (1974), ‘What Is It Like to Be a Bat?’ The Philosophical Review, Vol. 83, pp. 435-50.

Kathleen Akins (1993), ‘What Is It Like to Be Boring and Myopic?’ in B. Dahlbom, ed., Dennett and His Critics. 2nd ed. Cambridge, MA: Blackwell, pp. 124-60.

Session 4: Intentionalism

Required Reading

Alex Byrne (2001), ‘Intentionalism Defended’, Philosophical Review, Vol. 110, pp. 199-240.

Suggested Reading

Ned Block (1990), ‘Inverted Earth’, in J.E. Tomberlin, ed., Philosophical Perspectives, Vol. 4, Action Theory and Philosophy of Mind. Atascadero, CA: Ridgeview, pp. 53-79.

Amy Kind (2010), ‘Transparency and Representationalist Theories of Consciousness’, Philosophy Compass, Vol. 5, No. 10, pp. 902-13.

Session 5: The Objectivity of Mind

Required Reading

Mark Johnston (2007), ‘Objective Mind and the Objectivity of Our Minds’ Philosophy and Phenomenological Research, Vol. 75, No. 2, pp. 233-268.

Suggested Reading

Frank Jackson (1982), ‘Epiphenomenal Qualia’, Philosophical Quarterly, Vol. 32, pp. 127-36.

Ruth Garrett Millikan, ‘Biosemantics’, The Journal of Philosophy, Vol. 86, pp. 281-97.

Session 6: The Unity of Consciousness

Michael, Tye (2007), ‘The Problem of Common Sensibles’, Erkenntnis, Vol. 66, pp. 287-303.

Suggested Reading

David Hume, Treatise of Human Nature, Book 1, part iv, sect. 6.

Tim Bayne and David Chalmers (2003), ‘What Is the Unity of Consciousness?’ in A. Cleeremans, ed., The Unity of Consciousness: Binding, Integration, Dissociation, Oxford: Oxford University Press, pp. 23-58.
